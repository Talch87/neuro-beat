Bottom line. The VEB detection product is complete. A two-stage gated-ensemble cascade — a genuinely sparse high-recall screener (about 6,000 SynOps) on every beat, gating a 3-seed ensemble confirmer that runs only on the ~27% of beats it flags — meets all three targets on DS2 simultaneously: sensitivity 0.923, PPV 0.616, 23,385 SynOps/beat.

A single model cannot do this: it faces a real sensitivity–precision tradeoff (about 0.89/0.49 or 0.86/0.68, never both), which turned out to be calibration variance, not a capacity limit — a seed ensemble reaches the target corner, and the cascade delivers that ensemble accuracy within the energy budget by running it only on flagged candidates. VEB sensitivity holds ≥ 0.90 across three databases (DS2, SVDB, INCART) under one frozen operating point.

Supraventricular (SVEB) detection is a separate, data-limited problem: about 0.13 on a single lead, rising to 0.62 on 12-lead INCART, so the capacity exists but the data and lead count do not. It is now pursued by a dedicated SVEB specialist rather than by degrading the VEB operating point.

The SNN reaches VEB sensitivity close to the CNN with about a third of the parameters, but at lower VEB PPV. The gap in PPV between the SNN and the CNN is the discrimination problem the current objective targets. The CNN uses roughly 355k multiply-accumulates per beat, which is why it separates VEB from normal beats better and why matching it inside a 25k SynOps budget is hard.

Active investigation

Two changes from recent literature are being tested to raise VEB discrimination while lowering compute:

1. Low-timestep count-pooled encoder

The delta-encoded spikes are pooled into a small number of time bins (for example 32 instead of 256). This cuts the SNN's sequential loop length, which both speeds up training by about 16x and lowers SynOps per beat, helping the 25k budget.

2. RR-interval timing features

Three patient-normalized timing ratios (pre-RR, post-RR, and their ratio, each divided by the record's median RR) are fed into the hidden layer. Premature beats have a short pre-RR, which is a cue that beat shape alone does not carry. With a small hidden layer this roughly doubled VEB precision at 90% recall on DS2, from about 0.15 to about 0.32.

3. Hidden-layer capacity (the ceiling breaker)

Widening the hidden layer from 128 to 256 units, with denser input spikes, lifted VEB precision at 90% recall from about 0.32 to 0.68 on DS2. This is the change that cleared the 0.50 target. The cost is SynOps: a wider layer and denser spikes push the per-beat synaptic operations to about 108,000, well over the 25,000 budget. The current frontier search asks how much of that discrimination survives when the configuration is forced back under budget.

These are feasibility reads from a precision-recall reachability diagnostic, not final operating points. Final numbers use the val-locked protocol below and will replace the status board values when the runs finish.

How the numbers are kept honest

Inter-patient split. The model trains on DS1 patients and is tested on DS2 patients (the de Chazal split). Beats from a test patient never appear in training, so the numbers reflect new patients, not memorized ones.

No test-set tuning. The operating point (per-class decision thresholds) is chosen only on a validation holdout carved out of DS1, then frozen before touching DS2. The test set selects nothing. This is why the reported PPV is lower than tuned numbers seen in some papers, and why it is trustworthy.

Per-class metrics. About 89% of beats are normal, so overall accuracy is easy to inflate. Sensitivity and PPV are reported per class for VEB and SVEB, which is what actually matters for detecting arrhythmias.
